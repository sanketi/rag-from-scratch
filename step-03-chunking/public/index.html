Chunking — Split Documents for Retrieval

Before you can search a document with AI, you have to cut it into bite-sized pieces. This step shows you how — and why it matters.

Learn it in 5 steps

  1. Imagine a thick textbook. You can't memorize the whole thing for one test question. You make flashcards — one idea per card. Chunking is making flashcards from a document.
  2. Each flashcard = one chunk. In RAG, chunks get turned into embeddings (Step 02) and stored in a database (Step 04).
  3. When someone asks a question, the system finds the chunks that best match — like picking the right flashcards — and sends only those to the AI.
  4. Chunk size matters. Too big → the AI wades through junk. Too small → sentences get cut in half and lose meaning.
  5. Overlap helps. Repeating the last few words of chunk 1 at the start of chunk 2 so an idea never gets split across a boundary.

Strategy cheat sheet

Smart fixed-size

Best default. Aim for ~400 chars but break at sentence ends so you don't cut mid-thought.

Fixed-size

Cut every N characters exactly. Easy to understand, but can split "30" from "days" — try it to see the problem.

By paragraph

One chunk per blank-line-separated block. Great when your doc is already well-structured.

By sentence

One chunk per sentence. Very precise, but tiny chunks — more storage, more search noise.

Overlap

If chunk 1 ends with "...refund policy is 30 days" and chunk 2 starts with "30 days for returns", the shared overlap means neither chunk loses the full idea.
